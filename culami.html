CulAmi

A Smart Kitchen Experience using Touchless Interaction

M.S. HCI Capstone Project, Aug'14 - May'15

Description

Design for everyday life and the investigation of how technology can enter our lives in a seamless and pleasurable way have been some of the main goals of recent HCI research. With this vision in mind, my HCI Capstone Project, ‘CulAmi’, is a smart kitchen assistant designed for a better experience in the traditional kitchen. Derived from ‘culina amicum’, latin for ‘kitchen friend’, CulAmi is an app designed to reduce the overhead of cleaning your hands often just to be able to interact with multiple devices. CulAmi runs on any Android tablet/phone and is hooked up to a Leap Motion sensor. The app binds a recipe tool, music player, timer and oven control and enables users to perform simple, recurring tasks with these tools using their hands.




Design Process

Starting with requirement elicitation through surveys and informal data collection methods, the system ideation proceeded with the first round of participatory design sketches followed by low-fidelity paper prototypes, another round of feedback from real users and cognitive walk-through that finally lead to the development of the high-fidelity prototype. The project is currently in the testing phase and will be evaluated in a real world setting with users using the system prototype in a controlled setting to evaluate the impact and usefulness of this proposed technology.

Interactive Prototype

The mobile app side of the system was developed using Eclipse and Android SDK. Usability Evaluation

  • Recruited 6 participants for user testing
  • Task based scenario (four tasks)
  • Product briefing followed by a short, hands-on trial with the Leap Motion Controller
  • Post-task interview and SUS ratings.





Summary of Findings

  • Visually appealing user interface with a consistent experience.
  • Participants appreciated the product’s use case and applicability in existing kitchen spaces and agreed that they would use this product themselves.
  • The product was easy to learn and visual aids helped with certain gestures.
  • Simple and intuitive touchless gestures received favorable feedback from participants.
  • Participants needed a virtual tour and/or more visual aids to remember what gestures triggers what action.
  • A visual indicator is needed to help users understand if their hand is outside the Leap’s field of vision. Simple imagining this space was difficult and inaccurate.
