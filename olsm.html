OLSM Universal Dice

Physical Prototyping using Arduino Redboard

Prototyping Interactive Systems, Spring'15 IUPUI

Problem Space

A common practice seen amongst most tabletop games such as Dungeons and Dragons, Risk etc. is that players must deal with a number of different dice. We want to address the hassle of dealing with a collection of specialized dice. Introducing the “OLSM die”, the one universal die capable of replacing every other die ever used in a game. The OLSM die is a product concept that enables players to choose and roll any given die using just the one electronic die. Equipped with a LCD screen, button(s), a motion sensor and a speaker, the OLSM die enables the selection of a range of die (d4, d8, d10, d20 etc.) and simulated rolling of a die after the device is given a good shake.




Design Process

After several iterations of sketches and informal feedback, we made a physical prototype of the device that resembled something like a scepter. The idea was to be able to hold this device in one hand and use it more than just a dice in creative ways: this could potentially be used as a themed artifact and be swung around as a weapon or in style as one ‘rolls the die’. When we thought about the design of the universal die, we wanted to preserve essence of regular dice, as much as possible: playfulness, auditory feedback, and ease in number recognition. We also wanted to give it a completely new shape, which would be convenient to hold in hand and not easy to lose/drop. Thus, design of our die was inspired by the shape of traditional maracas. We envisioned that such a shape will intuitively call players to shake the device and also give it the appropriate affordance.




Physical Prototype

The components used:

  • a button for selecting the type of die, which was located for convenience on the device’s handle
  • an LCD for showing information to select the type of die, on what type of die is currently selected, and what is the current roll after it has been randomly generated
  • a flex sensor to recognize the shaking of the device
  • a speaker for either producing sound during the shaking of the device or for feedback after the roll has been generated
  • a few breadboards for housing the above components plus wires, resistors, and potentiometer.
  • a portable battery for power supply, which was connected to the red board.
